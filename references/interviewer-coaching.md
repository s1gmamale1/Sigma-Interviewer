# Interviewer coaching

How to run interviews that actually predict performance, and how to debias your own judgment. Grounded in selection-psychology research (citations below).

## The five highest-leverage habits

1. **Be structured.** Same questions, same order, anchored scoring. Structured interviews have roughly **2× the predictive validity** of unstructured chats, and rank near the top of all selection methods. This is the single biggest lever.

2. **Talk less.** Aim for **~20% interviewer / ~80% candidate**. Every minute you talk is signal you don't collect. After a question, **stay silent** — let them fill the gap; that's where real signal lives.

3. **Don't teach mid-interview.** Explaining the right answer feels helpful but destroys measurement — once you've said it, you can't tell if they knew it. Convert teaching into a *probe* ("how would you prevent that?") and save the lesson for a 2-minute debrief at the end (also leaves a great impression).

4. **Use dynamic assessment, standardized.** Pressuring a weak point is good — it's how you measure learnability — but do it with a **fixed hint-ladder** and grade **elevation per hint** (see `runsheet-15min.md`). Push with hints, never answers. This fixes the two real flaws of stress interviews: lack of standardization and unfairness to stress-sensitive people.

5. **Score to debias.** Rate each dimension **independently and immediately**, from evidence quotes, before discussing with anyone. This is the structural countermeasure to the **halo effect** (one strong impression — usually confident delivery — colors everything) and **confirmation bias** (forming a verdict early, then hunting for support). A second independent rater is the strongest single debias.

## Combine question types (best for juniors)
- **Behavioral** ("tell me about a time…") validates track record — ownership, specifics, problem-solving.
- **Situational** ("what would you do if…") reveals thinking and potential — ideal when the candidate has little work history.
Use both. Funnel each: broad → "why?" → "what would you do differently?"

## Question bank by trait
- **Real building:** "Walk me through something you built. What was *your* part? What broke, what did you do?"
- **AI fluency:** "Take your last task — exactly how did you use AI? Which model, why, what did your prompt look like, how did you verify it?"

## AI-workflow probe (ask every candidate)
- **AI fluency (required probe):** "Take your last task — exactly how did you use AI? Which model, why, what did your prompt look like, how did you verify it?"
- **English-as-collaboration probe:** "In what language do you actually code with AI? Give one example of a difficult bug you explained to AI and what you asked it to do."
- **Model/skill evidence probe:** "Show one example where AI gave a wrong result and what you did next."
- **Depth follow-up:** "What did you keep human-only in your process that AI could not do well yet?"
- **Learning agility:** "Something technical you taught yourself in the last 3 months — how, how long?" / "A mistake that changed how you work today." (Listen for reflection, feedback-seeking, behavior change.)
- **Discipline / punctuality:** "A stretch with several deadlines and not enough time — what did you actually do?"
- **Decision-making:** "A decision you made with incomplete information — how did you decide, what happened?"
- **Self-awareness / integrity:** "Rate yourself 1–10 at X — now convince me with a specific example," then probe the gap.
- **Coachability:** Give a small correction live and watch if they integrate it; "Feedback that was hard to hear — what did you do with it?"

## Common biases to watch in yourself
- **Halo / horn:** one trait (charm, a brand-name school) dominates the whole rating.
- **Confirmation:** deciding in the first minute, then collecting evidence to agree.
- **Affinity / similarity:** rating people like you higher.
- **Contrast:** judging a candidate against the previous one instead of the bar.
- **Recency/primacy:** over-weighting the first or last thing they said.
Countermeasure for all: anchored, per-dimension, evidence-based scoring done immediately.

## What's usually over-asked (cut it)
- The long company pitch mid-interview — move to the end or a one-pager; it eats signal time and primes answers.
- Re-asking the same question 4–5 times — one good probe + silence beats five rephrasings.
- Tool/OS trivia ("what is Linux?") — naming tools doesn't predict performance; replace with a work sample or a conceptual probe.
